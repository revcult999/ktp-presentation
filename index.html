Experience is really evidence. Evidence that you can solve problems, work with people, communicate clearly, use data, make decisions, and learn from feedback.

So, what is a KTP? It stands for Knowledge Transfer Partnership. In simple terms, it's a partnership between three sides: a business, a university, and a graduate associate.

It works for all three sides. For business, KTP gives access to cutting-edge knowledge — helping them develop new products, services and processes, boosting productivity and profitability. For academic institutions, it lets researchers apply their expertise to real-world challenges, strengthening industry links and driving impactful research. For graduates, it's a unique opportunity to do meaningful work, gain industry experience, lead a transformational project, and accelerate your career.

So it's not just a graduate job, and not just an academic research project. It sits somewhere in between — and that's what makes it interesting. You work on a real business problem, but you also get academic support, structured training, and a professional development budget.

For business students, KTP is a great example of how university learning moves into the real world — strategy, operations, data analysis and project management all coming together in one role.

So let me show you the project I'm actually working on. Wealmoor is a fresh-produce business — they import fruit and vegetables from growers around the world and supply major UK supermarkets.

The core mission: develop and deploy new approaches to global food supply chain management. In practice, that breaks into two things.

First — moving key perishable products from air freight to sea freight. Air is fast but expensive and very high carbon. Sea is far cheaper and greener, but the challenge is keeping produce fresh over a much longer journey.

Second — making the supply chain more resilient: coping with freight volatility, cutting emissions, and extending shelf life so the produce arrives in good condition.

These are the origin countries on the globe — Egypt, Kenya, India and Gambia — where we source things like green beans and Tenderstem broccoli. This is a genuinely global, real-world operations problem.

So what have we actually achieved? These are real numbers from a live commercial project, not a classroom case study.

On the Egypt green bean line, we took sea freight from just 9% of volume up to 69% in a single year — that's 37 tonnes up to 166 tonnes. Because sea freight is so much lower carbon, that delivered a 48% cut in CO₂e per tonne.

And critically, quality didn't suffer — qualifying waste actually fell from 19% to 9.7%. So we moved to the cheaper, greener option and reduced waste at the same time.

On Kenya Tenderstem broccoli, through postharvest trials we extended shelf life from 14 to 35 days and cut weight loss from 24% to under 1%.

The point for you: this is what "innovation" actually looks like day to day — testing, measuring, and turning a result into a business decision.

My KTP · Project Review Areas

So how do we actually make sea freight work? Extending shelf life isn't one single fix — it's the whole journey, from farm to fork. I look at four pillars.

One — Agronomical best practice. It starts at the farm: the right growing and harvesting conditions so produce begins the journey in the best possible state.

Two — Post-harvest handling & technology. What happens right after harvest — rapid cooling, the right processing — to slow down deterioration.

Three — Packaging for shelf-life extension. Things like modified atmosphere packaging that control the oxygen and CO₂ around the produce.

Four — Logistics solution. Inside the container: proper airflow, correct palletisation, and controlled-atmosphere transport to hold conditions steady across weeks at sea.

Get all four right together, and the produce survives the longer sea journey — that's what makes the air-to-sea switch possible.

The best career evidence is not a skill name — it is a story of action, context and impact.

When you look at graduate job descriptions, you see the same words again and again: problem solving, communication, teamwork, analytical skills, adaptability, leadership, business awareness.

But the important point: employers aren't just looking for those words on your CV. Almost everyone can write "I have strong communication skills" or "I'm a good team player." The real question is — can you prove it?

This is where evidence matters. In my KTP project, problem solving isn't something I claim — it's shown through analysing a real operational challenge, understanding constraints, testing solutions and thinking about impact.

Communication is shown through explaining data and findings to different audiences — academic supervisors, company colleagues, senior managers. Teamwork, through working with stakeholders across the business, university and supply chain. Data skills, through building tools and turning raw data into something people can actually use to make decisions.

So when you apply for internships, placements or graduate roles, don't just list your skills — turn them into evidence-based stories.

A stronger answer isn't "I'm good at teamwork." It's: "here's the situation, here's who I worked with, here's what I did, and here's the result."

That's what employers really want — not skills as words, but skills demonstrated through action, context and impact.
